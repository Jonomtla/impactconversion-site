01

You don't know why people buy (or don't)

I know how it is. Shipping products, designing new landing pages, coming up with new ads to test. It is exciting work, the work you enjoy, and it makes you feel like you are moving the needle. Customer research, on the other hand, sounds boring, and for the most part it is. But if you are writing headlines, objection-handling copy, or ads without first understanding what actually causes your customer to convert, you are stabbing in the dark.

How to spot it

Your page isn't converting, and you're unsure why

Good business is providing a solution to a problem someone has. If you do not understand the problem, or what motivates them to buy your solution, your business is running on an assumption. And the moment you started marketing the company, you crossed a line you cannot go back on: you can never be your target market again.

Opinion-based shipping

"It annoys me, so it must annoy customers" is spending money on an assumption. You see your website every day in ways your customers never do. Of course you are tired of the page you have seen a thousand times. It might be working, and you are changing it because you are bored of it.

Made-up messaging

If your headlines and product names come from a brainstorm in a board room, your messaging reflects what you believe is important. It will keep missing the actual motivations, anxieties, triggers, and desired outcomes of your buyers.

The fix: three ways to start, in order

1

Talk to your customers

Email your list and ask to jump on a call. Add an incentive, like a voucher giveaway, and aim for at least 5 to 10 conversations. Ask:

  • Who they are, and why they were interested in the first place
  • How they heard about you, and how hard it was to find a solution to their problem
  • Which competitors they considered, and what made them choose you instead
  • What almost stopped them from buying
  • Their biggest fear or concern with buying from you, and what got them over it
  • Whether the product met their expectations, and why

Then run the same questions as a survey to your list for volume. Do the calls first: they tell you which questions the survey should ask.

2

On-site surveys

Same idea, but in the moment, which gets you different answers. Use an on-site survey tool and ask at key points in the journey:

  • On purchase success: "What was the one thing that almost stopped you from purchasing today?" and, separately, "What convinced you to buy from us today?"
  • On landing pages (desktop on exit intent, mobile around average engagement time): "What appeals to you most about our products?", "What, if anything, holds you back from purchasing?", "What could we do to make your decision easier?"
  • On the homepage: "What was the purpose of your visit today?", "Were you able to complete it?", and the follow-up "why not?" when the answer is no.

Run one survey on the purchase journey at a time, for at least a week or about 100 valid responses per key segment.

3

User testing, if budget allows

Ten tests in total, five mobile and five desktop, on your main funnel. Define clear goals, qualify hard (100% target market, never purchased from you), focus on tasks before opinions, give minimal guidance, ask them to think aloud, and keep each session to 20 or 30 minutes on one or two core flows.

What to do with the research

Compile everything into one document and categorise it: motivations, values, frictions, anxieties, objections. Compare converters and non-converters. You are answering two questions: "what convinced customers to buy?" and "what almost stopped them?"

From there, theorise ways to amplify what convinces people and counter what stops them. Put the ideas in a spreadsheet and rank them by expected conversion impact against implementation difficulty. That ranked list is your testing roadmap for the next quarter.

A caution on AI analysis: the easy path is dumping the data into an AI tool and asking for insight. It works as a first pass, but the tools still make things up. Check the quotes are real before you base a landing page on them.

02

Weak value proposition (and not being consistent)

Have you ever seen a great ad, been almost sold on the product from the ad alone, then clicked through to a page that does not live up to the hype? It actively reduced your probability of purchasing. You could be making the same mistake.

The three versions of this mistake

The landing page doesn't match the ad

Testing different angles and claims in ads is fine. But if the landing page does not back up the claim that earned the click, conversion suffers. Symptoms: high bounce, low scroll below the fold, high exits from the pricing section, and survey answers showing confusion about the offer.

The page doesn't match the awareness stage

Problem-aware visitors get feature lists. Solution-aware visitors cannot see why you are different. Product-aware visitors cannot find the risk removal. The pattern: engagement with weak progression to checkout, plus feedback like "not sure if this solves my situation" and "what's the refund?"

There is no unique value proposition at all

If someone lands on your site, can they answer "who is this for?" and "why should I care?" in five seconds? Is your offer actually different, or do five other companies claim the same thing?

The car analogy: everyone knows what a car is. They know the problem (too far to walk) and the solution (a vehicle). The advertiser's job is to make them product aware: sell the specific car, in their price range, with the features they are in market for. Nobody selling cars re-explains walking. Match your message to what your buyer already knows.

A quick test

Walk someone new through your landing page and ask: What's in it for me? Why should I choose you over the alternative? Why is that a good thing? Can you prove it? If they cannot answer those, you have a value proposition problem.

The fix

1

Restore ad-to-page scent

When someone clicks an ad, they arrive with a specific promise in mind, and the landing page should feel like the same conversation. Echo the ad's key phrase in the H1 or first subhead line, keep the same angle and benefit, and repeat any incentive you offered. Running multiple angles? Swap the headline, proof, and CTA based on campaign parameters so every visitor sees the page that matches the promise they clicked.

2

Tune the hero to the awareness stage

  • Problem-aware visitors need to see you understand the problem and the cost of doing nothing before you promise the transformation.
  • Solution-aware visitors want your unique mechanism and why it beats the alternatives.
  • Product-aware visitors are deciding now: surface price, guarantee, outcomes, and make the CTA immediate.
  • Unaware visitors need the big-idea hook first, then the problem, then the outcome.
3

Clarify the UVP and prove it

State a single-sentence promise that answers who it is for, the outcome, the timeframe, and the most common objection. Use it as your H1 and repeat it in the title tag. Name your mechanism: the inputs that make you different, the process, and the measurable output versus alternatives. Quantify benefits wherever you legitimately can.

Then hold the evidence adjacency rule: every important claim sits next to proof within one scroll, on desktop and mobile. A testimonial, a stat, a recognisable logo, or a quick demo.

Validation checks

5-second test

Can a fresh user say who it is for and what they get?

Logo-swap test

Would the page read the same with a competitor's logo on it?

Scent check

Does the headline repeat the ad's key promise and phrasing?

03

The trust and authority gap

If your website does not earn people's trust, you might as well throw away half your customers. The factors that move trust run much wider than testimonials:

  • Does the site look visually trustworthy, like a professional entity?
  • Is there a real founder or team behind it, with a track record?
  • Are your guarantees specific and strong, or weak, or missing?
  • Are you running fake countdowns people can see through?
  • Do your testimonials back up the outcome you claim to deliver?
  • Do you have third-party reviews? Can a buyer find your about and contact pages easily?
  • Are recognisable payment methods visible near the CTA?
  • How many others have bought, and do you show it?

How to spot it

Low conversion rates

Obviously. But trust is the cause more often than most teams think.

Bounce patterns that hunt for trust

Design-related distrust bounces fast. Claim-related distrust engages, then searches the site for proof, then leaves.

Pre-sale inquiries

"Is this legit?", "Can I see case studies?", "Do you have a guarantee?" If support keeps hearing these, the page is not surfacing enough trust.

The fix

Testimonials, case studies, reviews

Use text and video, place them next to products and CTAs rather than at the bottom of the page, and keep them fresh.

Recognisable logos

Partner, client, or press logos at strategic points: homepage, landing pages, just before checkout. Only logos your audience will recognise.

Strong guarantees

Specific and actionable beats vague: "30-day no-questions-asked refund," stated prominently in plain language.

Hard data behind claims

Attribute numbers to sources, and visualise the key ones so they get seen.

Proof ladder, do and don't: do timestamp outcomes, include denominators ("12,400 customers" beats "thousands"), and link to third-party sources. Don't use stock headshots, vague praise, or "as seen in" logos that don't map to the offer. And kill dishonest countdowns: if you run real deadlines, show the real deadline and why it exists.

04

Above-the-fold fog

The term comes from newspapers: folded on the stand, only the top half showed, so publishers put their best work above the fold. On a landing page it is everything visible before scrolling, and 100% of the traffic that lands sees it. No other part of the page has that reach.

How to spot it

The 5-second test fails

Visitors cannot tell who it is for, what they get, or why it is different within five seconds.

No action, no scroll

Click maps show more attention on the navigation and logo than the primary CTA. Scroll depth is thin. Cold traffic wants detail before "Buy now" and cannot find it.

Weak or misplaced proof

Decorative stock imagery, no testimonial or stat near the fold, and low video play rates. Quick scrolls, quick exits.

The fix: what belongs above the fold

1

Your headline

The clear value proposition from killer 2. Who it is for and the outcome they get.

2

A subheadline that earns the claim

Expand on the headline and explain why you can do what you just claimed.

3

A primary call to action

Make the next step obvious. One action, not three.

4

Credibility indicators

At least one trust element visible without scrolling: proof, testimonial, or guarantee.

5

Key visuals

Obvious product photos or short video that reinforce the offer. Show the thing.

5-second test protocol: show the hero to ten fresh users and ask: who is this for, what do I get, what should I do next? Pass if at least seven of ten answer all three without scrolling. The fold is where testing pays back fastest, because every visitor sees it. Test messaging variations, risk reduction, and trust anchors here before anywhere else.

05

Friction everywhere: speed, forms, payments, logins

How to spot it

Slow pages

Load speed is the most common form of friction, and the most measurable. Mobile cold traffic feels it worst.

Abandonment and rage clicks

Cart abandonment at specific steps, rage clicks in session recordings, and people failing to find things through navigation or search.

Support tickets for simple things

Track support requests per 1,000 purchases. Rising tickets for basic tasks means the site is making people work.

The fix

1

No login before checkout

I have personally run tests where removing forced login before checkout was worth $300k+ a year. If you need accounts, create them after payment, not before.

2

Fix Core Web Vitals where it counts

Run PageSpeed Insights on your highest-traffic templates first. Compress media, defer non-critical scripts. Targets: LCP 2.5s or faster, CLS 0.1 or lower, INP 200ms or faster, measured with real-user data where possible.

3

Map the jobs your visitors came to do

Figure out whether the navigation gets people to their goal fast. Test menu structures against direct category routing, and check how many people actually use the nav and what for.

4

Checkout field audit

Ten or fewer visible fields for guest checkout, optional fields clearly marked, autofill enabled, correct mobile keyboards for email and numbers, inline validation as people type, guest checkout always available.

5

Geo-matched payments

Mirror the top payment methods for each market you sell into (Apple Pay, Google Pay, PayPal, BNPL, local options), show prices in the buyer's currency with tax handled the way they expect, and keep recognisable processor marks near the CTA.

06

Leaving money on the table post-purchase

The moment someone buys is when trust and motivation peak. If you do not extend that momentum, you cap lifetime value, mute referrals, and make every new-customer dollar work harder than it has to.

How to spot it

Weak repeat-purchase rate

90-day cohorts repurchase under 10% and the 12-month repeat rate never clears 20%. You are underserving existing fans.

Low average order value

Items per order hovers at 1.0 and order-bump attach sits in the low single digits. Buyers were never shown, or never trusted, a logical add-on.

Flat revenue per visitor

Traffic climbs, add-to-cart looks fine, but dollars per session will not budge. You are squeezing the same juice out of every first purchase and never compounding.

Stalled upsells

Post-purchase offers see short dwell and under 5% take. Support grumbles about pushy or confusing extras. The offer is mismatched or badly timed.

Quick diagnostics: pull 90, 180, and 365-day repurchase rates by month of first purchase, the bump and upsell attach rates, and tag refund and support themes to see what blocks the second sale.

The fix

1

Smart order bumps at checkout

A tightly related add-on priced at 15 to 30% of the main product, framed as completing the kit. Target a 15 to 25% attach rate. The maths stacks fast: a 20% attach on a $49 bump across 1,000 monthly orders is roughly $9.8k a month, from one change.

2

One-click post-purchase upsells

Immediately after payment, one yes-or-no offer with no card re-entry. Tight hook, benefit bullets, one social-proof tile. A 5 to 10% take is a good baseline; one client's post-purchase offer hit 26%.

3

48-hour activation sprint

A short email or SMS sequence that gets the product used: welcome with a clear next step, a getting-started nudge, a social-proof touch, a quick survey. Usage drives the second order.

4

Review and referral at the win moment

Trigger the ask at the first win with the product, not three minutes after checkout. Happy scores get the referral ask, low scores route to support.

07

No experimentation or measurement discipline

Testing is how you stop guessing. Without it, shiny redesigns and "best practices" become expensive coin flips, and variance can make a bad change look good for a day before it sinks performance for weeks.

But messy testing is not much better. Calling tests early creates false positives, and underpowered experiments create confidence without truth, so you scale the wrong ideas and stall. Proper testing de-risks build time, finds the cheapest path to lift, and compounds learning.

Quick definitions:
  • MDE: the smallest lift worth caring about
  • Power (aim for ~80%): whether your traffic can detect that lift
  • Statistical significance: the reason you do not call winners early
  • SRM: a broken traffic split. Pause and fix before reading anything.

How to spot it

Redesigns that fall flat

You ship a full new page and watch conversion fall once the novelty wears off, with no way to isolate which change caused it.

A win rate that makes no sense

You test, but wins are rare and inconsistent, because hypotheses are not grounded in research.

Uplifts that never reach revenue

Occasional green dashboards, but the revenue line barely moves: wrong metric, sample too small, or the change does not survive across devices and regions.

The fix

1

Check you have the volume, then test properly

Around 1,000 orders a month is where A/B test reads become reliable. Use a simple protocol: research, hypothesis, sample-size calculation, run, post-test analysis, log. And read impact across the full funnel (visit, add to cart, checkout, purchase), not a single click metric.

2

Prioritise ruthlessly

Score every idea by expected revenue impact, how confident the evidence makes you, and the effort to ship. Sort, and run the top items within capacity. Fewer tests, done right, raise both win rate and revenue.

Common pitfalls

Underpowered tests

Calculate the minimum detectable effect first, then wait for enough sample. Hope is not a sample size.

Novelty effects

Compare early and late cohorts. Do not ship day-one wins.

Broken splits

Check the traffic split matches what you set. If it is off, pause and fix before reading results.

Uneven device and geo mix

Segment before you ship. A win that only holds on desktop in one country is not a win.

Confounded changes

On lower traffic, isolate variables. A variant with five changes cannot tell you which one worked.

Where to start testing

  • High-friction areas of your landing pages: around the CTA and key decision points
  • The elements above the fold (killer 4): every visitor sees them
  • The homepage hero: nail the value proposition from killer 2
  • Navigation: can you get the visitor to done faster?
  • And every fix in this playbook: each one is a hypothesis worth testing rather than assuming

If you have low traffic

Bigger swings, not micro tweaks

Offer packaging, pricing, guarantees, and hero messaging. Use directional metrics (revenue per visitor, add-to-cart, AOV) rather than chasing significance.

Qualitative-heavy loops

Interviews, user tests, on-site polls. Smoke tests for demand: soft launches, waitlists, pre-sale pages with clear refund policies.

Longer windows, sequential ships

Extend test windows or batch releases, and watch cohort trends rather than day-to-day numbers.

Log everything

Document learnings in a simple log so decisions compound, win or lose. Twelve months in, the log is the asset.
